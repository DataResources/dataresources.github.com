Appendix A: Theoretical Comparison of Alternative Fit Perspective

 

Person-technology (P-T) fit perspective

Task-technology (T-T) fit perspective

Socio-technology (S-T) fit perspective

Key premise

Individuals live in an equilibrium relationship with their technological environment, and if this relationship is out of equilibrium, the misfit could lead to stressful outcomes (Ayyagari, Grover, & Purvis, 2011; Benlian, Klumpe, & Hinz, 2020).

The performance depends on an appropriate fit between task and technology (Goodhue & Thompson, 1995).

Only the technical and social systems work together and fit/align with each other, can the optimized outputs could be achieved  (Bostrom & Heinen, 1977).

Main independent constructs

Technical characteristics, personal characteristics, evaluation of P-T misfit

Task characteristics, technology characteristics

Technology, people and knowledge, skill, values, and attitudes

Main dependent constructs

Stressors, strain

Individual performance, system utilization

Performance

Prescriptive Statements

The individual’s perception of the degree of misfit between personal and technological characteristics can lead to stressors, resulting in strain and other negative outcomes.

Information technology is more likely to positively affect individual performance if its characteristics match the characteristics of tasks that users should perform (Goodhue & Thompson, 1995).

The technical system focuses on technology, task, and operations, whereas the social system emphasizes the relationship among people and their attributes such as attitude and skills (Wan et al., 2017). A successful system is the result of the simultaneous configuration of the technical and social aspects of the system (Bostrom & Heinen, 1977).

The degree of relevance to the context of this study

Technical characteristics can be contextualized into ESM characteristics, whereas personal characteristics cannot be contextualized into ESM usage.

Technological characteristics can be contextualized into ESM characteristics, whereas task characteristics cannot be contextualized into ESM usage.

The technical subsystem could be contextualized into ESM characteristics whereas the skill in the social subsystem could be contextualized into ESM usage because it is related to the skill level of the users using the ESM (Cai et al., 2018; Pasmore, Francis, Haldeman, & Shani, 1982)

Appendix C: Mixed-Methods Approach and Criteria[2]

Quality Aspects

Quality Criteria

Author’s Response to Venkatesh et al. (2013) Guidelines

Objective of mixed method approach

Development

This research consists of two stages: (1) qualitative study, including interviews to understand and identify some of the core ESM characteristics, and (2) quantitative study, namely a relatively large-scale survey. The qualitative study was applied to specify factors for model establishment and hypotheses argumentation, which was examined in the subsequent quantitative study.

 

Sequential less-dominant qualitative followed by a dominant quantitative investigation

The objectives and scope of the qualitative study using a set of interviews with employees who frequently use ESM are very limited; it is mainly to provide the foundation for quantitative research.

Design quality

Design adequacy

Qualitative interviews and limited documentary analysis were first used in this study, followed by a quantitative survey. This strategy of examining “raw” data in the phenomenon as a “prelude” to the larger scale quantitative study guaranteed that the proposed model examined using the quantitative research was related to the phenomenon of interest.

In such case, it attempted to take full advantage of two approaches by combining them together, in order to achieve deep insight into the phenomenon and the coverage breadth.

 

 

Qualitative

   Selecting suitable interviewers: The interviewers were employees who had regular interactions with ESM in their daily work and were thus regarded as suitable.

   Entering the field with credibility: The first author was mainly responsible for conducting the interviews. As this project was part of her doctoral research, it received a high degree of cooperation from the interviewees.

   Conducting the interviews: The interviews were based on the refined protocol and followed the principles of non-direction, flexibility, range, and specificity (Flick 1998).

 

Analytical adequacy

Qualitative

   Transcription of the fruitful and relevant interviews (Walsham 2006); application of the interview outline; other documents such as the screenshots of ESM(i.e., Enterprise WeChat) formed part of the qualitative database.

   The detailed qualitative analysis techniques and principles of theoretical engagement were iteratively applied in the interviews by the authors (Sarker et al., 2013).

   The authors labeled and relabeled the relevant concepts after the code was generated. The process was iterative and roughly similar to a continuous comparative analysis, which would end when the theoretical saturation occurred (Glaser and Strauss 1967).

   Although the concept of interrater reliability was not used, the identification and selection of concepts represent the consensus of all authors involved in the data collection and data analysis, which implies some form of convergence and reliability.

   Triangulating data from many interviews; making comparison of the responses, especially cross levels and departments.

   The extracted concepts are demonstrated by quotes may enhance the plausibility further.

   Concerning the exploratory nature of this investigation (getting discovery mainly through “raw” data) and the limited scope of the qualitative study, the concept of theoretical validity does not apply here.

 

 

Quantitative

   Justification of the selection of analytical techniques (that is, hierarchy regression analysis).

   489 sample size to ensure reasonable power.

   The survey was randomly distributed to avoid the bias in the selecting of the subjects.

Explanation quality

Qualitative inference

The identified constructs through the qualitative research were not only credible, but many of them are considered relevant in the prior literature.

 

Quantitative inference

Internal validity issues were dealt with by establishing a theoretically robust research model, reliable data collection process and reliable measurement methods, and appropriate statistical test.

Statistical conclusion validity was regarded as a special kind of internal validity, which is determined by guaranteeing construct validity, testing for CMB, and appropriate significance levels for tests.

External validity was determined to some extent by ensuring the samples of this study are highly representative by comparing the distribution of demographic variables of respondents in this study with those in previous ESM-related papers in TOP journals in the IS field.

 

Integrative inference

   Many of the identified constructs through qualitative study were empirically significant in the quantitative study.

   The R2 of the model was good. Especially when we incorporated the variables related to ESM characteristics, R2 increased and R2 differences were significant.

   It could be concluded that the model achieves a reasonable balance between parsimony and comprehensiveness, thus ensuring the integrative inference. The synergy between qualitative interviews and quantitative survey, which can be understood from the perspective of the qualitative study, increases the satisfaction level of integrative efficacy and efficiency.

Compatibility

The appraisal of ESM characteristics as related to promoting work and social activities.

Consistent with qualitative findings.

The compatibility of ESM supports the embedding of work- and social-related third-party software modules, thereby enabling employees to engage in work- and social-related activities via ESM.

Employees’ perceived compatibility of ESM could be conducive to improving their job performance (Sun et al., 2009); in addition, the social modules that are compatible with ESM could also promote the willingness of employees to use, thereby enhancing their social activities (Karahanna et al., 2006).

Interactivity

The appraisal of ESM characteristics as associated with enhancing three types of ESM usage.

Consistent with qualitative findings.

Interactivity means two-way communication, which is beneficial for employees to engage in working, social or hedonic activities via ESM.

The interactivity of ESM helps employees resolve differences and reach consensus in a timely manner, which is beneficial to promote work and social communication performance (Ou et al., 2014).

Customization

The appraisal of ESM characteristics as being positive for social and hedonic usage behaviors.

Consistent with qualitative findings.

Customization could largely satisfy the tastes and interests of employees, thereby beneficial for social and hedonic usage of employees.

The customization of ESM allows employees to get acquainted with colleagues who have common interests or relax through customized entertainment modules, thereby obtaining a certain sense of well-being (Bala and Venkatesh, 2013).

Transmission velocity

The appraisal of ESM characteristics as being positive for ESM usage behaviors.

Consistent with qualitative findings.

ESM has a relatively high transmission velocity, thus serving as a catalyst to completing work-related, social-related, and hedonic activities of employees.

Employees who use ESM usually respond quickly to incoming information, especially work-related ones, resulting in higher transmission velocity.

Appendix E: Interview Protocol

1)     What is your department and role in your company?

2)     Do you use enterprise social media frequently? If so, how long and how do you use them?

3)     Which modules of enterprise social media you use might affect your work? If so, how does it affect your work? Could you please share any specific experiences that are more impressive?

4)     Which modules of enterprise social media you use might affect your personal life? If so, how does it affect your life? Could you please share any specific experiences that are more impressive?

5)     What are the personal effects of unpleasant modules of enterprise social media?

6)     Do you have some suggestions for group leaders to manage the usage of enterprise social media more effectively and efficiently?

7)     Do you have some suggestions for the company who designed the enterprise social media?

8)     If the company cares about the well-being of employees and wants to make some improvements, what kind of suggestions would you like to give regarding the usage of enterprise social media?

Appendix N: Robustness Analysis

To evaluate the power of these hypothesis tests, we followed several procedures drawing on previous studies (Liu et al., 2016; Venkatraman, 1989; Vorhies & Morgan, 2003). First, we compared the regression models containing deviation from the identified ideal profile with those that contain deviation from another randomly selected “nonideal” baseline profile. Notably, we first selected the same number of employees as the top performers in each given ESM usage group in terms of job performance and well-being. Then, we used the randomly selected employees to calibrate a nonideal profile. Similarly, we calculated the deviation of the remaining employees to the nonideal profile by equation (1), as shown above. Finally, we substituted the nonideal profile deviation into the regression model to replace the ideal profile deviation for comparisons. As shown in the table below, the deviation from the nonideal profile has no significant effect on two outcome variables (e.g., job performance and well-being) for any ESM usage types, which provides further evidence surrounding the robustness of our results.

Secondly, we regarded the “fit” as a higher-order construct, taking all ESM characteristics and usage as its reflective indicators. Then, we constructed the model by linking the “fit” with job performance and well-being (Liu et al., 2016; Vorhies & Morgan, 2003). The loading range of all seven indicators, including the four identified ESM characteristics and the three ESM usage types were from 0.714 to 0.825, which is significant. In addition, the relationship between “fit” and job performance (b=0.679 p < 0.001) and well-being (b=0.376 p < 0.001) was significant, respectively. These results suggest that all ESM characteristics and ESM usage should be tested as an interdependent set to form outstanding outcomes (e.g., job performance and well-being). It further supports the conceptualization of “fit” between ESM characteristics and ESM usage.

Finally, we compared the outcomes (e.g., job performance and well-being) deviating from two different ideal profiles in the table of Online Appendix 6. The first one is called “within ESM usage group ideal profile” (abbreviated as within-group in the table below) from employees within the same ESM usage group. The other is called “cross ESM usage group ideal profile” (abbreviated as cross-group in the table below), which is from all employees not considering the ESM usage type. As shown in Table 6, the “within-group” model had a higher R2 (R2 = 0.318) than the “cross-group” (R2 = 0.246). Meanwhile, the absolute value of the coefficients in the former group (b= -0.326) was higher than that of the latter group (b= -0.201). Similarly, regarding the “within-group” and “cross-group” for well-being, the R2 value and absolute value of the coefficient in the former group (R2 = 0.152, b= -0.232) were higher than those in the latter group (R2 =0.116, b= -0.103). Overall, this robustness test shows that our identified ideal profile can accurately identify employees who can realize better outcomes (e.g., job performance and well-being) through configuration that meets the requirements of the ESM usage type (Venkatraman and Prescott 1990, Zaefarian, Henneberg & Naudé 2013).
